BIRD TAIL GROWTH AND EVOLUTION

The long tail of extinct dinosaurs evolved rapidly during the Early Cretaceous Period into the short, terminally fused tail present in all living birds. The growth of living organisms can inform the evolution of anatomical structures and how paleontologists interpret fossils. However, the tail developmental sequence in living birds is unknown, which hinders proper identification of intermediate fossils during the Mesozoic transition to short tails. Dr. Dana Rashid, other colleagues, and I found that bird tail vertebrae change radically with growth. As birds reach maturity, the last four to six vertebrae fuse into a compound bone called the pygostyle. Vertebral side projections mineralize long after hatching. Therefore, our study warrants re-interpretation of a previously-described transitional species, Zhongornis haoae, and the non-avian dinosaur tail in amber from Myanmar. Zhongornis, as a juvenile, might not have yet formed a pygostyle, and the dinosaur tail, which is also immature, might be avian. To read more about this study, check our paper in the PUBLICATIONS page!


BIRD PRELAYING EGG ROTATION

In 1951, J. R. G. Bradfield radiographed a 180° short-axis rotation of a hen’s egg inside the uterus about an hour before the egg is laid. The egg sinks ventrally during rotation and ascends back with its blunt end pointing caudally. Does this rotation exists in birds other than the chicken? If yes, does it shape bird pelvic morphology?
