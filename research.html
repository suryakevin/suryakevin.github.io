BIRD TAIL DEVELOPMENT AND EVOLUTION

The long tail of Mesozoic dinosaurs rapidly evolved into the short, terminally-fused tail of living birds. Two fossil specimens (Zhongornis haoae and the dinosaur tail preserved in Burmese amber) exhibit a transitional tail morphology: short and unfused. However, our study on bird tail development warrants re-interpretation of these transitional species. Bird tail vertebrae change radically with growth. As birds reach maturity, the last four to six vertebrae fuse into a compound bone called the pygostyle. Vertebral side projections mineralize long after hatching. Zhongornis, as a juvenile, might not have yet formed a pygostyle, and the dinosaur tail, also immature, might be avian. Check out our paper in the PUBLICATIONS page.


BIRD PRELAYING EGG ROTATION

Bird pubic bones separated at least 95 million years ago. An open pelvic canal, however, might not ease egg laying because it allowed for a 180° short-axis egg rotation in the uterus. Researchers first deduced this rotation over 100 years ago in the chicken and a few other species. Prelaying egg rotation may exist in all birds and select for a wide pelvic canal; a narrow one could cause complication. We show, using a comparative approach, no evidence for universal rotation across birds, assuming it shapes pelvic morphology.

Collaborators: Isabelle Brenes, Jacob Gardner, Nic Rawlence, Alan Tennyson, Lázaro Viñola López, Chris Organ, and David Varricchio.

GENOTYPE-PHENOTYPE ASSOCIATION

Phylogenetic trees with branch lengths scaled to the amount of molecular evolution (substitution) explain trait data better than those to time. Molecular evolution matches trait evolution. For comparative studies, researchers should use molecular branch lengths whenever possible.

Collaborator: Chris Organ

FOSSIL RECORD BIAS IN MACROEVOLUTIONARY ANALYSES

Fossil record bias confounds analyses of species dispersal through space and time. Using the tetrapod water-land transition as a case study, Jacob Gardner, Chris Organ, and I propose a proxy for fossil sampling bias that incorporates geographic information. Estimated ancestral locations and dispersal rates of early tetrapods can be explained by insufficient fossil record in regions except for Southern Euramerica.
